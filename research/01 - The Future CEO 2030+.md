# The Future CEO 2030+

## What the Research Actually Says—and Where It Falls Short

The consulting industry has produced a cottage industry of "future leadership" research. Every major firm has published frameworks, competency models, and trend reports on what CEOs will need in 2030 and beyond. Strip away the branding, and a striking convergence emerges: most are saying remarkably similar things, and most are looking backward while claiming to look forward.

This document synthesises the credible research, identifies where consensus exists, surfaces the genuine tensions and contradictions, and—critically—identifies the gaps that matter for assessing leaders who must navigate genuinely discontinuous change.

---

## The Emerging Consensus: Where Research Converges

### 1. Technical Competence Is Necessary but Insufficient

Every major study reaches the same conclusion: deep functional expertise and business fundamentals remain essential, but they no longer differentiate. The marks of successful leaders, according to AACSB/CEMS research across 20 global companies, "will be their ability to understand the implications of data, facilitate change, drive impact, and relate well to and influence others."

This represents a shift from **knowing** to **applying**—from expertise as destination to expertise as raw material for creative problem-solving.

### 2. Learning Agility Has Become the Meta-Capability

Across the research, learning agility emerges as the capability that enables all others. Russell Reynolds identifies it as one of four defining characteristics of AI-ready leadership. Korn Ferry's research shows learning agility predicts promotability and post-promotion performance. The Center for Creative Leadership has developed dedicated assessment instruments.

But there's a catch: the academic evidence base is thinner than the consulting hype suggests. A systematic review in *Frontiers in Psychology* (2022) notes that "research on the malleability of learning agility is scarce" and that the construct benefits from being broken into narrower, more assessable dimensions.

> **The honest position:** Learning agility matters. It predicts things we care about. But the research is earlier-stage than the confident frameworks imply, and most organisations assess it poorly or not at all.

### 3. AI/Technology Fluency Is Rapidly Becoming Non-Negotiable

The acceleration here is dramatic. KPMG's 2025 CEO Outlook shows:
- 71% of CEOs cite AI as a top investment priority (up from 64% in 2024)
- 67% expect AI ROI in 1-3 years (previously 63% expected 3-5 years)
- 23% identify AI and digital literacy as essential leadership skills
- 77% agree workforce AI readiness will impact organisational prosperity

Egon Zehnder now calls AI skills assessment "foundational, not niche" and has developed a seven-point scale for evaluating how leaders understand, apply, and lead AI initiatives.

Russell Reynolds puts it starkly: "Those paying attention to AI will outperform equally qualified peers who aren't. It's as essential as engineers adopting calculators when they first came out."

### 4. The "Soft" Skills Are Actually the Hard Ones

Emotional intelligence, cultural intelligence, empathy, self-awareness—these appear in every framework. The AACSB/CEMS research explicitly states that "deeply human skills" including "resilience and adaptability" and "emotional intelligence and humility" are essential.

Jacob Morgan's "Notable Nine" framework, derived from interviews with 140 CEOs, includes "Yoda" (emotional intelligence, specifically empathy and self-awareness) as one of five essential skills.

The research is unambiguous: in a world where AI can do the analysis, human capabilities—judgment, relationship, meaning-making—become the differentiators.

### Egon Zehnder: AI Leadership Assessment Framework (2025)

Distinguishes two archetypes:

**AI Transformers (Business Roles):** Drive adoption and integration of AI across strategy and culture through vision, influence, and execution—not necessarily technical expertise.

Assessed on:
- Leading change in the face of AI disruption
- Using data to make strategic decisions and prioritise AI use cases
- Understanding customers through AI-enhanced insights
- Collaborating across functions to embed AI into business processes

**AI Builders (Technical Roles):** Design, develop, and deploy AI solutions that power business innovation and operational efficiency.

Assessed on:
- Leading change and fostering innovation within technical teams
- Using data and machine learning to inform strategic decisions
- Collaborating across departments to integrate AI into business processes
- Mentoring and developing technical talent to scale AI capabilities

## The Honest Critique: Where the Research Falls Short

### 1. Most Frameworks Are Backward-Looking

The research methodology—interviewing current CEOs about what future leaders will need—has an obvious flaw: incumbents extrapolate from their own experience. The resulting frameworks are variations on "what made us successful, plus technology."

This is particularly problematic when the question is discontinuous change. Asking railroad executives in 1910 what transportation leaders would need in 1930 would not have surfaced "willingness to cannibalise your business for aviation."

### 2. The AI Treatment Remains Superficial

Despite the emphasis on AI and digital fluency, the frameworks treat it as a capability domain rather than a fundamental restructuring of how organisations operate. "Being comfortable with AI" is not the same as understanding:
- Agent orchestration and hybrid human/machine workforces
- Different AI paradigms and their governance implications (Anthropic vs OpenAI philosophies)
- How AI changes the nature of decision-making, not just its speed
- The accountability structures required when algorithms make or inform consequential choices

### 3. Cultural Context Is Acknowledged but Not Integrated

The research acknowledges that leadership effectiveness is culturally contingent, but the frameworks remain fundamentally Western. The emphasis on individual traits, hero leadership, and universal competencies sits uncomfortably with collectivist contexts, relationship-based cultures, and state-directed economies.

For Indonesian SOEs operating across Southeast Asia, a framework built on American Fortune 500 assumptions may actively mislead.

### 4. Transformation Capability Is Under-Theorised

Every framework mentions change, agility, and adaptation. Few grapple seriously with:
- What distinguishes leaders who successfully transform from those who don't
- Whether transformation capability is innate or developable
- How to assess transformation potential rather than past transformation success
- The specific cognitive and emotional demands of leading through discontinuity

### 5. Assessment Validity Is Assumed, Not Demonstrated

The frameworks imply that the competencies can be assessed, but the evidence base for predictive validity is thin. Learning agility research shows promise, but broader "future leader" assessments lack longitudinal validation. We are better at describing what we think matters than at measuring it accurately.

## What Actually Predicts Transformation Success?

The research on transformation capability is more modest but more rigorous than the grand competency frameworks.

### Learning Agility: The Best-Evidenced Predictor

Academic research (Lombardo & Eichinger, 2000; multiple subsequent studies) shows learning agility significantly predicts:
- Supervisory ratings of promotability
- Performance following promotion
- Long-term career success

The construct typically includes:
- Mental agility (cognitive flexibility, comfort with complexity)
- People agility (understanding and relating to diverse others)
- Change agility (willingness to lead change, comfort with ambiguity)
- Results agility (delivering under challenging, first-time conditions)

However, *Cogn-IQ.org* analysis (2025) suggests learning agility is "substantially explained by general mental ability (GMA) and the Big Five personality trait of openness to experience"—constructs with longer research histories and better-validated measures.

### Cognitive Flexibility Under Pressure

The ability to update mental models when evidence contradicts them—and to do so under stress—appears consistently in transformation research. This is distinct from general intelligence; it's specifically about:
- Recognising when existing approaches are failing
- Generating alternative interpretations of the same evidence
- Shifting strategy without identity threat

### Tolerance for Ambiguity (Genuine, Not Performed)

Many leaders claim comfort with ambiguity but demonstrate the opposite under pressure—defaulting to false certainty, premature closure, or analysis paralysis. Genuine tolerance for ambiguity shows up as:
- Sustained decision-making in low-information environments
- Ability to act while acknowledging uncertainty
- Not requiring external validation to proceed

### Self-Awareness: The Foundation

The research consistently surfaces self-awareness as foundational. Leaders who accurately assess their own strengths, limitations, and impact are better positioned to adapt. Those with inflated self-assessments systematically overestimate their transformation readiness.

## Implications for Assessment

### What to Assess

Based on the research, a future-readiness assessment should prioritise:

1. **Learning Agility** — particularly cognitive flexibility and comfort with ambiguity
2. **AI Fluency** — not just comfort with tools, but understanding of paradigms, governance, and workforce implications
3. **Systems Thinking** — ability to see connections across technology, talent, strategy, and culture
4. **Self-Awareness Accuracy** — correlation between self-assessment and external assessment
5. **Transformation Track Record** — but interpreted carefully, with attention to context and role

### What to Deprioritise

1. **Past performance in stable conditions** — poor predictor of future performance in discontinuous conditions
2. **Credential accumulation** — degrees, certifications, and CV entries that signal compliance more than capability
3. **Confidence and charisma** — often inverse predictors of transformation success
4. **Self-reported adaptability** — nearly all leaders claim it; few demonstrate it

### Assessment Method Implications

- **Psychometrics** for cognitive ability, openness, and learning agility components
- **Structured behavioural interviews** for transformation track record, interpreted with context
- **Simulation/scenario exercises** for real-time cognitive flexibility and decision-making under ambiguity
- **AI-assisted analysis** for pattern detection across large candidate populations
- **360-degree feedback** for self-awareness accuracy (comparing self-ratings to others' ratings)
