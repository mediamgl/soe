# The Western Leadership Model Problem

## Why Fortune 500 Competency Frameworks May Actively Mislead

The leadership competency frameworks that dominate global talent assessment were built primarily from American Fortune 500 executives. They encode specific cultural assumptions about what leadership is, how authority operates, and what effectiveness looks like. These assumptions are not universal truths—they are Western, individualist, and increasingly anachronistic even in their home context.

Applying these frameworks uncritically to Indonesian SOE leaders is not merely suboptimal—it may actively mislead, identifying the wrong leaders and missing the right ones.

---

## The Hidden Premises of Western Leadership Models

Academic research has identified six premises embedded in Western leadership theory that "may not be fully applicable to non-Western contexts":

### 1. Leader-Centred

Western models focus on the individual leader as the primary unit of analysis. The leader is protagonist; followers are supporting cast. This framing underemphasises collective leadership, relational dynamics, and the contextual factors that shape what leaders can accomplish.

**In Indonesian context:** SOE leadership operates within dense networks of political, familial, and institutional relationships. Individual heroic leadership is less relevant than navigation of these networks.

### 2. Male-Dominated

Leadership research historically derived from male executive populations. Competencies often encode masculine-coded traits—assertiveness, independence, decisive action—while undervaluing feminine-coded capabilities like relationship-building, collaborative sense-making, and care.

**In Indonesian context:** Gender dynamics in Indonesian business differ from Western norms. Frameworks that implicitly reward masculine leadership styles may miss capable leaders who operate differently.

### 3. Universal Traits

Western frameworks assume that certain traits predict leadership effectiveness universally—strategic thinking, decisiveness, communication skills. This ignores evidence that trait effectiveness is culturally contingent.

**In Indonesian context:** Traits valued in American contexts (directness, individual achievement orientation, comfort with conflict) may be less effective or even counterproductive in Indonesian relational contexts.

### 4. Task-Relationship Balance

Western models typically separate task focus from relationship focus, suggesting leaders should balance both. This framing misses cultures where tasks and relationships are inseparable—where getting work done *is* relationship maintenance.

**In Indonesian context:** Indonesian business operates through relationships. The Western separation of "task" and "relationship" may not map onto how work actually happens.

### 5. Quantifiable Performance

Western frameworks emphasise measurable outcomes—revenue growth, market share, ROI. This privileges what can be counted over what matters but cannot be easily measured.

**In Indonesian context:** SOE performance includes public service obligations, regional development, employment creation, and political stability. These are harder to quantify but no less important.

### 6. Individualist Values

Western leadership models assume individual agency, personal accountability, and meritocratic advancement. They underemphasise collective identity, hierarchical loyalty, and obligation-based relationships.

**In Indonesian context:** Indonesian culture scores high on collectivism. Individual achievement orientation may be less motivating than family, community, or national contribution.

## The Power Distance Problem

Power distance—the degree to which less powerful members of a society accept and expect that power is distributed unequally—fundamentally shapes leadership dynamics.

### Western Frameworks Assume Low Power Distance

Most leadership competency frameworks were developed in low-to-moderate power distance cultures (US, UK, Northern Europe). They assume:
- Leaders should be accessible and approachable
- Decision-making should incorporate input from subordinates
- Hierarchy is a necessary structure, not a source of meaning
- Challenging authority is acceptable and even valued

### High Power Distance Contexts Work Differently

Research on leadership in high power distance contexts (including Indonesia) shows:
- Authoritarian and paternalistic leadership styles may be effective, not merely tolerated
- "Transformational leadership" effects diminish when followers have high power distance orientation
- The meaning of "participation" differs—consultation can be ritualistic rather than substantive
- Direct challenge to authority may be culturally inappropriate regardless of its merit

### The Indonesian Reality

Indonesia scores high on power distance. This doesn't make authoritarian leadership automatically good—but it means:
- Leadership effectiveness criteria differ from Western assumptions
- Assessment methods must account for different expectations
- "Empowerment" means something different than in Western contexts
- The same behaviour signals different things

## Paternalistic Leadership: The Blind Spot

Western frameworks typically treat paternalistic leadership as problematic—patriarchal, disempowering, anachronistic. But research in Asian contexts reveals a more nuanced picture.

### Benevolent Paternalism

Benevolent paternalistic leadership combines:
- Clear authority and direction-setting
- Genuine care for followers' wellbeing (including family)
- Protection and support in exchange for loyalty
- Long-term relationship orientation

In high power distance contexts, this style can generate:
- High trust and psychological safety (within the hierarchy)
- Strong loyalty and commitment
- Effective execution of direction
- Stability and predictability

### The Western Critique vs. Asian Reality

| **Western View** | **Asian Reality** |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Paternalism is patronising | Paternalism can be caring |
| Authority should be minimised | Authority provides security |
| Empowerment means autonomy | Empowerment means protection |
| Individual growth is paramount | Collective welfare matters more |

### Assessment Implications

A framework that penalises paternalistic leadership will:
- Systematically disadvantage leaders who are effective in Asian contexts
- Reward Western-style individualism that may be less effective locally
- Miss genuine leadership capability because it looks different

## Transformational Leadership: The Overrated Export

Transformational leadership—inspiring vision, intellectual stimulation, individual consideration, idealised influence—has been treated as universally positive. The research is more equivocal.

### What the Research Actually Shows

A study of 403 followers in Russian organisations (another high power distance context) found:
- Transformational leadership was negatively associated with emotional exhaustion (good)
- But under conditions of high individual power distance orientation, this association diminished
- The relationship between leadership style and outcomes depends on follower expectations

### The Fit Problem

Transformational leadership assumes:
- Followers want to be inspired
- Vision matters more than direction
- Individual consideration is valued
- Intellectual challenge is welcome

In high power distance, collectivist contexts:
- Followers may want clarity more than inspiration
- Direction may matter more than vision
- Collective consideration may matter more than individual
- Intellectual challenge may feel like disrespect

### The Hybrid Requirement

The research suggests that effective leaders in high power distance contexts may need to combine:
- Clear authority and direction (paternalistic elements)
- Genuine care and protection (benevolent elements)
- Some inspirational and developmental focus (transformational elements)

A pure transformational style may be less effective than a hybrid adapted to context.

## The State-Owned Enterprise Dimension

SOE leadership differs from private sector leadership in ways that compound the Western model problem.

### Different Accountability Structures

| **Private Sector (Western Model)** | **SOE Reality** |
|------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Accountable to shareholders | Accountable to multiple political stakeholders |
| Clear performance metrics | Ambiguous, competing objectives |
| Market determines success | Political survival determines success |
| Relatively clear authority | Authority negotiated with government |

### Different Success Criteria

SOE leaders must balance:
- Commercial performance
- Public service obligations
- Political relationships
- National development contribution
- Employment and regional stability
- Family and patronage obligations

Western frameworks optimised for shareholder value creation miss most of what makes SOE leaders effective.

### Different Career Dynamics

SOE leadership positions are often:
- Politically allocated
- Term-limited by political cycles
- Subject to sudden change with government transition
- Part of broader political careers

Assessing "strategic thinking" or "drives results" without understanding these dynamics produces meaningless results.

## What Actually Matters in Indonesian SOE Context

Based on the research and contextual analysis, effective Indonesian SOE leadership likely requires:

### 1. Political Navigation Capability

The ability to:
- Maintain relationships across political factions
- Survive government transitions
- Balance competing stakeholder demands
- Create space for operational autonomy within political constraints

### 2. Relational Intelligence

Understanding:
- How decisions actually get made (often outside formal structures)
- Which relationships enable and constrain action
- How to build trust across hierarchical and network boundaries
- The meaning of obligation and reciprocity

### 3. Contextual Judgment

Knowing:
- When to exercise authority directly vs. through consensus
- How to appear strong while maintaining flexibility
- When Western management practices apply and when they don't
- How to adapt external ideas to local context

### 4. Long-Term Orientation

Capacity for:
- Building institutional capability beyond personal tenure
- Investing in relationships that pay off over decades
- Patience for transformation timelines beyond political cycles
- Legacy thinking over short-term results

### 5. Adaptive Authority

Ability to:
- Be appropriately directive in contexts that expect it
- Be appropriately participative when it builds commitment
- Switch styles based on situation and stakeholder
- Maintain authority while enabling subordinate initiative

## Assessment Implications

### What to Avoid

1. **Direct application of Western frameworks** — They encode wrong assumptions
2. **Penalising paternalistic elements** — These may be effective locally
3. **Rewarding pure transformational style** — It may be less effective in context
4. **Measuring against Western CEO benchmarks** — Wrong reference population
5. **Ignoring political navigation** — Central to SOE effectiveness

### What to Build

1. **Contextually-grounded competency definitions** — What does "strategic thinking" mean in Indonesian SOE context?
2. **Hybrid leadership models** — Combining paternalistic, transformational, and adaptive elements
3. **Relational and political assessment** — Capabilities Western frameworks ignore
4. **Local validation** — Test against actual Indonesian SOE outcomes, not Western assumptions
5. **Stakeholder-informed criteria** — What do Indonesian stakeholders actually value in SOE leaders?

---

## The Honest Position

Western leadership frameworks are not worthless—they capture real dimensions of effectiveness that apply across contexts. But they are insufficient and potentially misleading for Indonesian SOE assessment.

The task is not to reject Western models but to:
1. Surface their hidden assumptions
2. Identify where those assumptions don't apply
3. Supplement with contextually-relevant dimensions
4. Validate against Indonesian outcomes, not Western benchmarks

Any assessment that simply applies Korn Ferry or DDI frameworks to Indonesian SOE leaders will produce systematically biased results. We need a more sophisticated approach.
