# The Generational Workforce Shift

## Leading People Who Don't Want to Be Led

For the first time in history, five generations are working simultaneously. Millennials now hold senior leadership positions. Gen Z constitutes a growing share of the workforce. Each generation brings different expectations about work, authority, and meaning. Leaders who approach the workforce as a homogeneous group will fail to engage, motivate, and retain the talent they need.

This document examines what the generational shift means for leadership and what capabilities Indonesian SOE leaders need to navigate it.

---

## The Five-Generation Workforce

### Current Workforce Composition

| **Generation** | **Birth Years** | **Age in 2026** | **Workforce Status** |
|----------------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Silent Generation | 1928-1945 | 81-98 | Largely retired |
| Baby Boomers | 1946-1964 | 62-80 | Retiring, some still active |
| Generation X | 1965-1980 | 46-61 | Senior leadership |
| Millennials | 1981-1996 | 30-45 | Moving into senior roles |
| Generation Z | 1997-2012 | 14-29 | Entering workforce |

### The Leadership Implication

CEOs now must lead:
- Boomers who may work under younger managers
- Gen X navigating organisational change
- Millennials taking on senior responsibility
- Gen Z with fundamentally different expectations

## Generational Differences That Matter

### Relationship with Authority

| **Generation** | **Authority Orientation** |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| Boomers | Respect for positional authority; earned through tenure |
| Gen X | Skeptical of institutions; pragmatic about authority |
| Millennials | Prefer collaborative leadership; question unnecessary hierarchy |
| Gen Z | Expect to be heard regardless of position; authority earned through competence |

**Leadership implication:** Hierarchical authority alone won't motivate younger generations. Leaders must earn respect through competence and authenticity.

### Relationship with Work

| **Generation** | **Work Orientation** |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Boomers | Work defines identity; career as primary focus |
| Gen X | Work-life balance pioneers; pragmatic about employment |
| Millennials | Purpose and meaning important; work must have value beyond pay |
| Gen Z | Work as part of life, not life itself; flexibility non-negotiable |

**Leadership implication:** "Work hard and you'll be rewarded" motivates differently across generations. Purpose, flexibility, and meaning matter more to younger workers.

### Relationship with Feedback

| **Generation** | **Feedback Orientation** |
|----------------|-------------------------|
| Boomers | Annual review is sufficient; uncomfortable with constant feedback |
| Gen X | Regular feedback appreciated; values direct communication |
| Millennials | Continuous feedback expected; grew up with constant measurement |
| Gen Z | Real-time feedback assumed; digital native expectations |

**Leadership implication:** Performance management systems built for annual review cycles don't meet younger generations' expectations.

### Relationship with Technology

| **Generation** | **Technology Orientation** |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| Boomers | Adapted to technology; may resist new platforms |
| Gen X | Technology as tool; comfortable but not immersed |
| Millennials | Digital natives; integrated technology into life |
| Gen Z | Technology is invisible; AI and automation are assumed |

**Leadership implication:** Leaders less fluent in technology lose credibility with younger workers. Digital literacy is baseline expectation.

## The KPMG Finding

KPMG's 2025 CEO Outlook highlights the challenge:

> "Labour market shifts and demographic changes—particularly an ageing workforce—are seen by 88% of leaders as having moderate to high impact on recruitment, retention and culture. Nearly a third (30%) of CEOs highlight growing generational gaps on key future skills, while a quarter (24%) are worried about the number of employees retiring, coupled with a lack of skilled workers to replace them."

### The Dual Challenge

**1. Ageing workforce:** Knowledge and expertise walking out the door through retirement

**2. Skills gap:** Younger workers lack institutional knowledge; older workers lack new skills

**3. Cultural tension:** Generational expectations creating friction

**4. Management challenge:** Multi-generational teams requiring different approaches

## Leading Without Traditional Authority

### The Shift

Traditional leadership relied on:
- Positional authority
- Information asymmetry (leader knows more)
- Career control (leader determines advancement)
- Organisational loyalty

These mechanisms weaken with younger generations:
- They question positional authority
- Information is democratised
- Career mobility reduces leverage
- Loyalty is earned, not assumed

### What Works Instead

| **Old Mechanism** | **New Mechanism** |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Authority from position | Authority from competence and character |
| Information control | Transparency and inclusion |
| Career as leverage | Development as attraction |
| Assumed loyalty | Earned commitment |
| Command and control | Coaching and enabling |

### The Leadership Capability

Leaders must be able to:
- Influence without relying on position
- Build trust through authenticity
- Demonstrate competence that earns respect
- Create development opportunities
- Provide meaning and purpose

## The Indonesian Context

### Cultural Factors

Indonesia's high power distance culture traditionally supports:
- Respect for authority and seniority
- Hierarchical decision-making
- Deference to elders and leaders
- Indirect communication

### Generational Tension

Younger Indonesian workers are:
- Exposed to global norms through digital media
- Less willing to accept traditional hierarchy
- More likely to question authority
- Seeking purpose and meaning in work

### The SOE Challenge

SOEs face particular generational challenges:
- Aging workforce with institutional knowledge
- Limited attraction for young talent vs. private sector
- Traditional cultures that may not appeal to Gen Z
- Need to modernise while retaining stability

## Capability Requirements for Multi-Generational Leadership

### 1. Generational Intelligence

Understanding:
- What different generations value
- How to communicate across generations
- What motivates each generation
- Where generational tensions arise

### 2. Adaptive Leadership Style

Ability to:
- Adjust approach based on audience
- Provide directive leadership when needed
- Coach and develop when appropriate
- Give autonomy where earned

### 3. Inclusive Decision-Making

Capacity to:
- Include diverse voices regardless of age
- Create psychological safety for input
- Value contribution over tenure
- Build consensus across generations

### 4. Development Orientation

Commitment to:
- Growing people at all levels
- Creating career pathways
- Knowledge transfer between generations
- Continuous learning culture

### 5. Purpose Articulation

Ability to:
- Connect work to meaning
- Articulate organisational purpose
- Help individuals find purpose
- Create culture of meaningful work

## Scenarios for Assessment

### Scenario 1: The Generational Conflict

Your team includes a senior Boomer expert and a younger Gen Z analyst. They're in conflict about how to approach a project—the Boomer values experience and proven methods; the Gen Z values innovation and fresh thinking. How do you handle it?

**Assessing:** Generational intelligence, conflict navigation, inclusive leadership

### Scenario 2: The Attraction Challenge

Your SOE struggles to attract young talent who prefer private sector or tech companies. How do you make the organisation attractive to Gen Z while maintaining what works for older employees?

**Assessing:** Generational understanding, cultural adaptation, employer brand thinking

### Scenario 3: The Knowledge Transfer

A key expert is retiring in 12 months. Their knowledge isn't documented and younger colleagues don't have the same expertise. How do you ensure knowledge transfer?

**Assessing:** Development orientation, knowledge management, succession thinking

## The Honest Assessment

Most SOE leaders:
- Built careers in traditional hierarchical environments
- May not understand what younger generations want
- Lead with positional authority they earned through tenure
- Have limited experience with Gen Z workforce

### What This Means

- Generational leadership capability cannot be assumed
- Assessment must explicitly surface generational intelligence
- Development may be needed even for otherwise capable leaders
- Some leaders may not be able to adapt
