# Leading Indicators vs Lagging Credentials

## The Problem with Assessing What Leaders Have Done

Traditional executive assessment focuses on track record: What has this leader accomplished? What roles have they held? What results have they delivered? This approach has a fundamental flaw: past performance in stable conditions predicts almost nothing about future performance in discontinuous conditions.

For Indonesian SOE leaders facing unprecedented transformation, we need assessment that surfaces leading indicators—what predicts capability to handle novel challenges—rather than lagging credentials that merely document past success.

---

## The Predictive Validity Problem

### What Track Record Actually Predicts

Past performance predicts future performance **when:**
- The future environment closely resembles the past environment
- The capabilities required remain stable
- The context factors (culture, stakeholders, resources) are similar
- The challenges are variations on familiar themes

Past performance predicts almost nothing **when:**
- The environment is discontinuously different
- Required capabilities shift fundamentally
- Context factors change dramatically
- The challenges are genuinely novel

### The Transformation Paradox

Leaders selected for current-state excellence often fail at transformation because:
- Their pattern-matching applies obsolete solutions
- Their confidence comes from expertise that may be irrelevant
- Their identity is tied to capabilities that need to change
- Their success was earned in conditions that no longer apply

**The paradox:** The more successful a leader was in the past, the harder it may be for them to adapt to a fundamentally different future.

## Lagging Credentials: What We Typically Assess

### Career Progression
- Titles held
- Speed of advancement
- Size of organisations led
- Scope of responsibility

**The problem:** Career progression reflects political skill, organisational fit, and success in past conditions. It may not reflect capability for future challenges.

### Results Delivered
- Financial performance
- Operational metrics
- Strategic achievements
- Transformation "experience"

**The problem:** Results are always contextual. A leader who delivered results with talented subordinates, favourable markets, and supportive boards may fail when those conditions change. Attribution is nearly impossible.

### Credentials Accumulated
- Educational pedigree
- Certifications
- Board memberships
- Industry recognition

**The problem:** Credentials signal compliance with established norms, not capability for innovation. They may indicate ability to navigate existing systems, not ability to create new ones.

### Experience Claimed
- "Led transformation" at previous company
- "Built the team that..."
- "Delivered X% growth..."

**The problem:** Self-reported experience is unreliable. Leaders routinely claim credit for results they didn't drive and distance themselves from failures they caused. Reference checking helps but doesn't solve the attribution problem.

## Leading Indicators: What Actually Predicts

### 1. Learning Agility

The ability to learn from experience and apply learning to new situations. Research shows it predicts:
- Supervisory ratings of promotability
- Performance following promotion
- Long-term career success

**How to assess:** 
- Structured behavioural interview about past learning experiences
- Psychometric instruments measuring cognitive flexibility and openness
- Simulation exercises requiring real-time adaptation

**What to look for:**
- Evidence of changing approach based on feedback
- Seeking unfamiliar experiences rather than comfortable ones
- Willingness to appear uncertain or incompetent while learning
- Pattern of taking on stretch assignments

### 2. Cognitive Flexibility

The ability to switch between different concepts, adapt to new information, and think about multiple concepts simultaneously.

**How to assess:**
- Scenario exercises requiring belief revision
- Case studies with conflicting information
- Real-time problem-solving under changing conditions

**What to look for:**
- Updates conclusions when evidence contradicts initial hypothesis
- Generates multiple interpretations of ambiguous data
- Maintains effectiveness when rules change mid-task
- Doesn't anchor too strongly on first information

### 3. Self-Awareness Accuracy

The correlation between how leaders see themselves and how others see them. Research shows self-awareness is foundational for adaptation.

**How to assess:**
- Compare self-assessment with 360-degree feedback
- Look for convergence/divergence on key dimensions
- Assess response to discrepant feedback

**What to look for:**
- Low divergence between self and other ratings (accurate self-perception)
- Appropriate humility about limitations
- Genuine curiosity about others' perceptions
- History of seeking and acting on feedback

### 4. Tolerance for Ambiguity

The ability to function effectively without clear information, defined outcomes, or established procedures.

**How to assess:**
- Decision-making exercises with incomplete information
- Observe behaviour when certainty is unavailable
- Assess comfort with "good enough" decisions

**What to look for:**
- Makes decisions without requiring complete information
- Maintains composure when plans don't unfold clearly
- Doesn't force premature closure or false certainty
- Acts despite uncertainty rather than waiting for clarity

### 5. Growth vs. Fixed Mindset

The belief that capabilities can be developed through effort versus the belief that capabilities are fixed traits.

**How to assess:**
- Structured interview about response to failure
- Psychometric assessment of implicit theories of ability
- Observation of response to challenge

**What to look for:**
- Treats failures as learning opportunities, not identity threats
- Seeks challenge rather than avoiding it
- Responds to difficulty with effort, not withdrawal
- Gives others credit for growth and development

### 6. Systemic Thinking

The ability to see connections, anticipate second-order effects, and understand complex interdependencies.

**How to assess:**
- Complex case studies requiring stakeholder analysis
- Scenario planning exercises
- Questions about unintended consequences of past decisions

**What to look for:**
- Considers multiple stakeholders and their interactions
- Anticipates effects beyond the immediate
- Recognises when solutions create new problems
- Understands feedback loops and dynamic systems

## The Assessment Implications

### What to Assess Heavily

| **Leading Indicator** | **Weight** | **Method** |
|-----------------------|------------|-----------|
| Learning agility | High | Psychometrics + behavioural interview |
| Cognitive flexibility | High | Scenario-based simulation |
| Self-awareness accuracy | High | 360 comparison + response to feedback |
| Tolerance for ambiguity | Medium-High | Decision exercises under uncertainty |
| Growth mindset | Medium | Interview + psychometrics |
| Systemic thinking | Medium | Case analysis |

### What to Assess Lightly

| **Lagging Credential** | **Weight** | **Use** |
|------------------------|------------|---------|
| Career progression | Low | Context only |
| Past results | Low-Medium | Understand approach, not predict future |
| Educational credentials | Low | Entry screening only |
| Industry experience | Low | Domain knowledge, not capability |
| Claimed transformation experience | Low | Explore for learning, not credit |

### What to Actively Question

- **High confidence** — Often inverse predictor of transformation success
- **Smooth career progression** — May indicate political skill over substance
- **"Always succeeded"** — May indicate risk avoidance or favourable conditions
- **Industry insider status** — May indicate entrenchment in old models

## The Attribution Problem

Past results are nearly impossible to attribute accurately:

### What Inflates Leader Credit
- Talented subordinates who delivered despite leadership
- Favourable market conditions during tenure
- Predecessor's investments reaching maturity
- Supportive board and stakeholder environment
- Luck and timing

### What Obscures Leader Contribution
- Difficult conditions that masked good decisions
- Time lag between decisions and outcomes
- Collective contributions attributed to individuals
- Political dynamics affecting perception

### The Honest Position

We can never fully attribute past results to individual leaders. What we can assess:
- **Process quality:** Did the leader make good decisions even when outcomes were uncertain?
- **Learning demonstrated:** Did the leader improve based on experience?
- **How they handled adversity:** Did difficulty reveal character and capability?
- **What others say:** Do subordinates, peers, and superiors attribute success to this leader?

## Potential Over Past Performance

Russell Reynolds identifies "potential over past performance" as a defining characteristic of AI-ready leadership. The logic:

### Why Past Performance Is Insufficient
- AI changes what capabilities matter
- Transformation requires new approaches
- Experience with old methods may impede new learning
- Future looks less like past than ever before

### What Potential Looks Like
- Demonstrated learning in novel situations
- Curiosity about emerging technologies and methods
- Comfort with experimentation and iteration
- Willingness to be a beginner again

### How to Assess Potential
- Focus on trajectory, not position
- Assess rate of learning, not accumulated knowledge
- Look for stretch assignments, not comfortable promotions
- Evaluate response to novelty, not performance on familiar tasks

## Application to Indonesian SOE Assessment

### What to Deprioritise

1. **Political career progression** — May reflect patronage more than capability
2. **SOE tenure** — Longevity may indicate entrenchment
3. **Claimed transformation results** — Attribution is impossible
4. **Industry reputation** — May reflect old-model success
5. **Educational pedigree** — Signals compliance, not capability

### What to Prioritise

1. **Learning evidence** — How have they changed their approach over time?
2. **Novel situation response** — How do they handle genuinely unfamiliar challenges?
3. **Self-awareness** — Do they accurately perceive their own capabilities?
4. **Ambiguity navigation** — Can they function without certainty?
5. **Systems perspective** — Do they understand interconnections?

### The Assessment Shift

| **From** | **To** |
|----------|--------|
| What have you accomplished? | How have you learned from experience? |
| What results did you deliver? | How did you handle situations with unclear outcomes? |
| What credentials do you have? | How do you approach unfamiliar challenges? |
| What's your track record? | What's your trajectory? |
| What do you know? | How quickly do you learn? |

---

## The Conversation Shift

### Traditional Assessment Questions
- "Tell me about your greatest achievement."
- "What results did you deliver in your last role?"
- "How did you turn around that business?"

### Leading Indicator Questions
- "Tell me about a time you changed your approach because it wasn't working."
- "Describe a situation where you had to make a decision with incomplete information."
- "What's something you believed strongly that you no longer believe?"
- "Tell me about a failure and what you learned from it."
- "When have you been wrong about something important?"
