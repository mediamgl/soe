# The Capability Architecture

## Dimensions and Definitions

This document presents the capability framework for assessing Indonesian SOE leaders' readiness for 2030+ transformation. It defines what we measure, how capabilities cluster, how they're weighted, and what each dimension means in operational terms.

This is the IP core—the framework that distinguishes our approach from generic competency models.

---

## Framework Overview

### Architecture Structure

The framework comprises four capability clusters, each containing specific dimensions:

```
TRANSFORMATION READINESS FRAMEWORK

├── ADAPTIVE CAPACITY (30%)
│   ├── Learning Agility
│   ├── Cognitive Flexibility
│   ├── Tolerance for Ambiguity
│   └── Self-Awareness Accuracy

├── FUTURE LEADERSHIP (25%)
│   ├── AI Fluency
│   ├── Hybrid Workforce Capability
│   ├── Systems Thinking
│   └── Generational Intelligence

├── CONTEXTUAL NAVIGATION (25%)
│   ├── Political Acumen
│   ├── Stakeholder Orchestration
│   ├── Cultural Adaptability
│   └── Long-Term Orientation

└── TRANSFORMATION EXECUTION (20%)
    ├── Change Leadership
    ├── Institutional Building
    ├── Governance Capability
    └── Results Under Ambiguity
```

### Weighting Rationale

| **Cluster** | **Weight** | **Rationale** |
|-------------|------------|---------------|
| Adaptive Capacity | 30% | Foundation for all else; predicts transformation success |
| Future Leadership | 25% | What makes 2030+ different from today |
| Contextual Navigation | 25% | Indonesia SOE-specific requirements |
| Transformation Execution | 20% | Gets things done in change conditions |

## Cluster 1: Adaptive Capacity (30%)

The foundational capabilities that enable leaders to navigate discontinuous change. Without these, other capabilities cannot be effectively deployed in transformation contexts.

### 1.1 Learning Agility (8%)

**Definition:** The ability to learn from experience and apply that learning effectively to new, first-time situations.

**Components:**
- Mental agility: Comfort with complexity and ambiguity in thinking
- People agility: Skilled at understanding and relating to diverse others
- Change agility: Willingness to lead change initiatives; curious and experimental
- Results agility: Delivers results in tough, first-time situations
- Self-awareness: Knows own strengths, limitations, and blind spots

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Seeks unfamiliar experiences and stretch assignments
- Changes approach based on feedback and results
- Comfortable appearing uncertain while learning
- Applies lessons from one domain to different contexts

**What LOW looks like:**
- Relies on proven approaches even when they don't work
- Avoids situations where expertise is lacking
- Defensive about limitations
- Repeats mistakes across situations

**Assessment methods:** Psychometrics + structured behavioural interview

---

### 1.2 Cognitive Flexibility (8%)

**Definition:** The ability to update mental models when evidence contradicts them, adapt thinking to new information, and shift between different conceptual frameworks.

**Components:**
- Belief revision: Updates conclusions when evidence changes
- Multiple perspective taking: Generates alternative interpretations
- Adaptive response: Maintains effectiveness when rules change
- Non-anchoring: Doesn't over-weight first information

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Says "I was wrong about that" when evidence warrants
- Considers multiple explanations for same data
- Adjusts strategy mid-course without identity threat
- Weighs new information appropriately vs. prior beliefs

**What LOW looks like:**
- Defends initial conclusions despite contrary evidence
- Sees situations through single lens
- Doubles down when approach isn't working
- Anchors on first information received

**Assessment methods:** Scenario exercises requiring belief revision

---

### 1.3 Tolerance for Ambiguity (7%)

**Definition:** The ability to function effectively without clear information, defined outcomes, or established procedures—and to make decisions when certainty is unavailable.

**Components:**
- Decision-making under uncertainty: Acts without complete information
- Composure: Maintains effectiveness when plans don't unfold clearly
- Appropriate closure: Doesn't force premature certainty
- Action orientation: Acts despite uncertainty

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Makes "good enough" decisions when perfect information is unavailable
- Comfortable with provisional conclusions
- Doesn't paralyse or force false certainty
- Communicates uncertainty appropriately

**What LOW looks like:**
- Delays decisions waiting for certainty
- Becomes anxious or erratic when situation is unclear
- Imposes false certainty to reduce discomfort
- Avoids situations with unclear outcomes

**Assessment methods:** Decision exercises under genuine uncertainty

---

### 1.4 Self-Awareness Accuracy (7%)

**Definition:** The correlation between how leaders perceive themselves and how others perceive them—the accuracy of self-assessment.

**Components:**
- Strength awareness: Knows what they're genuinely good at
- Limitation awareness: Knows where they're weak
- Impact awareness: Understands effect on others
- Feedback integration: Updates self-view based on input

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Self-assessment aligns with 360-degree feedback
- Acknowledges limitations without defensiveness
- Seeks feedback and acts on it
- Adjusts behaviour based on impact awareness

**What LOW looks like:**
- Significant gap between self-view and others' view
- Blind to limitations or overly self-critical
- Dismisses or avoids feedback
- Unaware of impact on others

**Assessment methods:** 360 comparison + structured reflection interview

## Cluster 2: Future Leadership (25%)

Capabilities specifically required for the 2030+ environment that most current leaders lack—AI integration, hybrid workforce management, systems complexity, and generational dynamics.

### 2.1 AI Fluency (7%)

**Definition:** Understanding of AI capabilities, limitations, paradigms, and governance requirements sufficient to lead AI-integrated organisations.

**Components:**
- Capability understanding: Knows what AI can and cannot do
- Paradigm awareness: Understands different AI approaches (Anthropic vs. OpenAI)
- Orchestration concepts: Grasps multi-agent coordination
- Governance thinking: Understands AI accountability requirements

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Articulates AI capabilities and limitations accurately
- Discusses different AI paradigms knowledgeably
- Thinks about AI governance proactively
- Personally uses AI tools regularly

**What LOW looks like:**
- Vague understanding of what AI does
- Treats AI as monolithic technology
- No awareness of governance requirements
- Delegates all AI thinking to technical teams

**Assessment methods:** Discussion + demonstrated AI usage

---

### 2.2 Hybrid Workforce Capability (6%)

**Definition:** Ability to lead organisations where significant work is performed by AI agents and automation alongside human workers.

**Components:**
- Work allocation design: Can structure human/AI task division
- Mixed team leadership: Manages humans working with AI
- Algorithmic accountability: Maintains responsibility for AI outputs
- Ethical automation reasoning: Makes principled automation decisions

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Thinks strategically about human vs. AI work allocation
- Designs oversight for AI decision-making
- Considers employment and ethical implications
- Builds governance for algorithmic work

**What LOW looks like:**
- Views AI as tools, not workforce components
- No framework for AI accountability
- Doesn't consider automation ethics
- Unprepared for hybrid teams

**Assessment methods:** Scenario exercises + strategic discussion

---

### 2.3 Systems Thinking (6%)

**Definition:** Ability to understand complex interdependencies, anticipate second and third-order effects, and see how components interact within larger systems.

**Components:**
- Interdependency recognition: Sees connections others miss
- Effect anticipation: Predicts downstream consequences
- Stakeholder mapping: Understands actor relationships
- Feedback loop awareness: Recognises dynamic system behaviour

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Considers ripple effects of decisions
- Sees how changes in one area affect others
- Maps complex stakeholder dynamics
- Anticipates unintended consequences

**What LOW looks like:**
- Addresses problems in isolation
- Surprised by downstream effects
- Oversimplifies stakeholder dynamics
- Doesn't see feedback loops

**Assessment methods:** Complex case analysis

---

### 2.4 Generational Intelligence (6%)

**Definition:** Understanding of different generational values, expectations, and motivations—and ability to lead effectively across generations.

**Components:**
- Generational understanding: Knows what different generations value
- Communication adaptation: Adjusts style for audience
- Authority navigation: Leads without relying solely on position
- Purpose articulation: Connects work to meaning

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Understands generational differences
- Adapts leadership approach by audience
- Earns respect through competence, not position
- Creates purpose and meaning effectively

**What LOW looks like:**
- One-size-fits-all leadership approach
- Relies on positional authority
- Frustrated by younger worker expectations
- Dismisses generational differences

**Assessment methods:** Structured interview + scenario

---

## Cluster 3: Contextual Navigation (25%)

Capabilities specific to Indonesian SOE context—political navigation, stakeholder complexity, cultural dynamics, and long-term institutional orientation.

### 3.1 Political Acumen (7%)

**Definition:** Ability to navigate complex political environments, maintain relationships across factions, survive transitions, and create operational space within political constraints.

**Components:**
- Political landscape reading: Understands power dynamics
- Relationship maintenance: Builds and maintains political relationships
- Transition survival: Navigates government changes
- Space creation: Creates autonomy within political constraints

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Maintains relationships across political factions
- Survives government transitions
- Creates room for operational independence
- Balances political demands with institutional needs

**What LOW looks like:**
- Aligned too closely with single faction
- Vulnerable to political transitions
- Captured by political demands
- Unable to create operational space

**Assessment methods:** Structured interview + reference checking

---

### 3.2 Stakeholder Orchestration (6%)

**Definition:** Ability to manage complex, competing stakeholder demands—balancing ministry, Danantara, employees, customers, regulators, and public expectations.

**Components:**
- Stakeholder mapping: Understands all relevant stakeholders
- Interest balancing: Navigates competing demands
- Communication management: Maintains appropriate relationships
- Coalition building: Creates alignment across stakeholders

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Clear understanding of stakeholder landscape
- Balances competing interests effectively
- Maintains trust across stakeholder groups
- Builds coalitions for important initiatives

**What LOW looks like:**
- Blind to key stakeholders
- Captured by single stakeholder group
- Creates enemies unnecessarily
- Unable to build cross-stakeholder support

**Assessment methods:** Case discussion + reference checking

---

### 3.3 Cultural Adaptability (6%)

**Definition:** Ability to operate effectively across Indonesian cultural contexts while adapting to international standards and expectations.

**Components:**
- Local cultural fluency: Navigates Indonesian contexts
- International standard awareness: Understands global expectations
- Adaptive code-switching: Adjusts approach by context
- Bridge building: Connects local and global

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Effective across Indonesian cultural contexts
- Understands international business expectations
- Switches between contexts fluidly
- Bridges local and global effectively

**What LOW looks like:**
- Limited to single cultural context
- Unaware of international expectations
- Rigid approach across contexts
- Cannot bridge local and global

**Assessment methods:** Cross-context scenario discussion

---

### 3.4 Long-Term Orientation (6%)

**Definition:** Capacity for decade-long thinking, building beyond personal tenure, and maintaining strategic patience despite political cycle pressures.

**Components:**
- Horizon extension: Thinks beyond immediate term
- Legacy orientation: Builds for successors
- Strategic patience: Maintains course despite pressure
- Institution over individual: Prioritises institutional health

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Makes decisions with 10-year horizons
- Builds capability beyond own tenure
- Resists short-term political pressure
- Prioritises institutional over personal

**What LOW looks like:**
- Focused on current political cycle
- Extracts rather than builds
- Capitulates to short-term pressure
- Prioritises personal over institutional

**Assessment methods:** Structured interview + track record analysis

---

## Cluster 4: Transformation Execution (20%)

Capabilities to actually deliver transformation—not just think about it. Leading change, building institutions, governing effectively, and delivering results without clear playbooks.

### 4.1 Change Leadership (5%)

**Definition:** Ability to lead others through transformational change—creating vision, building commitment, managing resistance, and sustaining momentum.

**Components:**
- Vision articulation: Creates compelling change narrative
- Commitment building: Enrolls others in change
- Resistance management: Handles opposition constructively
- Momentum sustainment: Maintains energy through long change

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Creates compelling vision that motivates
- Builds genuine commitment to change
- Addresses resistance without escalation
- Maintains momentum through difficulties

**What LOW looks like:**
- Imposes change without enrollment
- Creates compliance, not commitment
- Escalates resistance unnecessarily
- Loses momentum when difficulties arise

**Assessment methods:** Track record interview + scenario

---

### 4.2 Institutional Building (5%)

**Definition:** Ability to create and strengthen organisational capabilities that persist beyond individual leaders—structures, processes, culture, and talent.

**Components:**
- Capability development: Builds organisational skills
- Structure design: Creates effective organisational forms
- Culture shaping: Influences how work gets done
- Succession building: Develops future leaders

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Leaves organisations stronger than found
- Builds capabilities that persist
- Shapes culture constructively
- Develops strong successor pipeline

**What LOW looks like:**
- Extracts from rather than builds institutions
- Creates dependency on self
- Ignores culture
- No succession development

**Assessment methods:** Track record analysis + reference checking

---

### 4.3 Governance Capability (5%)

**Definition:** Ability to design and operate effective governance structures—accountability, oversight, reporting, and board engagement—appropriate for transformed organisations.

**Components:**
- Governance design: Creates effective oversight structures
- Board engagement: Works productively with boards
- Accountability clarity: Establishes clear responsibility
- Transparency operation: Maintains appropriate visibility

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Designs appropriate governance for context
- Engages board constructively
- Creates clear accountability
- Operates transparently

**What LOW looks like:**
- Governance as compliance, not value
- Board relationship adversarial or captured
- Unclear accountability
- Opacity where transparency needed

**Assessment methods:** Discussion + reference checking

---

### 4.4 Results Under Ambiguity (5%)

**Definition:** Ability to deliver outcomes in conditions of uncertainty, incomplete information, and changing requirements—where the playbook doesn't exist.

**Components:**
- Unclear-path execution: Delivers without clear roadmap
- Adaptive delivery: Adjusts approach as conditions change
- Resource mobilisation: Marshals resources in uncertain contexts
- Outcome focus: Maintains focus on results despite ambiguity

**What HIGH looks like:**
- Delivers in first-time situations
- Adjusts execution as learning occurs
- Finds resources in constrained conditions
- Stays focused on outcomes

**What LOW looks like:**
- Paralysed without clear plan
- Rigid execution despite changing conditions
- Unable to mobilise in novel situations
- Process focus over outcome focus

**Assessment methods:** Track record interview + scenario

## Scoring and Interpretation

### Dimension Scoring

Each dimension scored 1-5:

| **Score** | **Meaning** |
|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | Significant gap; unlikely to develop |
| 2 | Below requirement; development needed |
| 3 | Meets basic requirement; development helpful |
| 4 | Above requirement; strength |
| 5 | Exceptional; role model capability |

### Cluster Scoring

Weighted average of dimension scores within cluster.

### Overall Readiness

Weighted average of cluster scores.

### Readiness Categories

| **Score Range** | **Category** | **Interpretation** |
|-----------------|--------------|-------------------|
| 4.0-5.0 | Transformation Ready | Can lead major transformation |
| 3.5-3.9 | High Potential | Ready with targeted development |
| 3.0-3.4 | Development Required | Significant development needed |
| 2.5-2.9 | Limited Readiness | Major gaps; may not develop |
| <2.5 | Not Ready | Fundamental misalignment |
