# Governance in the Hybrid Era

## Accountability When Decisions Are AI-Assisted or AI-Made

Traditional corporate governance assumes human decision-makers with human accountability. When a CEO makes a bad decision, we know who to hold responsible. When AI systems make or inform consequential decisions, accountability becomes ambiguous. Leaders who cannot navigate this ambiguity will create governance gaps that expose their organisations to risk.

This document examines the governance challenges of hybrid human/AI organisations and what capabilities SOE leaders need to address them.

---

## The Accountability Challenge

### Traditional Accountability

In traditional organisations:
- Humans make decisions
- Decisions can be traced to individuals
- Individuals can explain their reasoning
- Accountability flows through hierarchy
- Boards oversee management; management oversees employees

### The AI Complication

When AI is involved:
- Who is accountable for AI-informed decisions?
- Who is accountable for AI-made decisions?
- How do you audit algorithmic reasoning?
- What oversight is appropriate at what level?
- How does the board oversee AI it doesn't understand?

### The Accountability Gap

Many organisations have:
- AI systems making consequential recommendations
- No clear accountability for AI outputs
- Limited ability to audit AI reasoning
- Boards with minimal AI literacy
- Governance structures designed for human-only organisations

## Layers of AI Decision-Making

### Layer 1: AI as Tool

**Description:** Humans use AI to do tasks faster (writing, analysis, search)

**Accountability:** Remains with the human using the tool

**Governance requirement:** Training on appropriate use, quality control

**Example:** Executive uses AI to draft communications

### Layer 2: AI as Advisor

**Description:** AI analyses data and makes recommendations that humans approve

**Accountability:** Human who approves the recommendation

**Governance requirement:** Understanding of AI limitations, validation processes

**Example:** AI recommends investment allocation; committee approves

### Layer 3: AI as Delegate

**Description:** AI makes decisions within defined parameters without human approval

**Accountability:** Ambiguous—designer, operator, or organisation?

**Governance requirement:** Clear boundaries, monitoring, override capability

**Example:** AI approves routine transactions under threshold

### Layer 4: AI as Agent

**Description:** AI pursues goals autonomously, making decisions humans don't review

**Accountability:** Highly ambiguous

**Governance requirement:** Robust safety constraints, outcome monitoring, kill switches

**Example:** AI agents managing supply chain optimisation

### The Governance Escalation

As AI moves from tool to agent, governance complexity increases exponentially:
- More decisions are made without human review
- Accountability becomes harder to establish
- Oversight requires technical sophistication
- Risk of unintended consequences grows

## Governance Framework for AI Decisions

### 1. Decision Classification

Every AI-involved decision should be classified:

| **Category** | **Description** | **Required Oversight** |
|--------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Routine | Low consequence, reversible | Monitoring, sampling |
| Significant | Moderate consequence | Human review before action |
| Consequential | High impact, hard to reverse | Senior approval required |
| Strategic | Fundamental to mission | Board or executive decision |

### 2. Accountability Assignment

For each AI system:
- **Sponsor:** Executive responsible for the system's existence
- **Operator:** Person/team responsible for day-to-day function
- **Validator:** Who confirms outputs are appropriate
- **Escalation path:** Who handles problems

### 3. Audit Capability

Organisations must be able to:
- Explain why AI made specific decisions
- Identify patterns in AI errors
- Trace outcomes back to inputs
- Demonstrate compliance with policy

### 4. Override Mechanisms

Every AI system needs:
- Clear conditions for human override
- Technical capability to stop or change AI behaviour
- Communication channels to raise concerns
- Protection for those who escalate issues

## Board Oversight of AI

### The Board's Challenge

Most boards have:
- Limited AI expertise
- No framework for AI oversight
- Reliance on management representations
- Difficulty evaluating AI risk

### Board AI Responsibilities

Boards should:
- Understand organisational AI strategy
- Review major AI deployments
- Receive regular AI risk reporting
- Ensure appropriate governance exists
- Hold management accountable for AI outcomes

### Board Composition Implications

From Russell Reynolds: "Companies are bringing on more AI-savvy directors, either with experience in future-facing businesses or in strategic product and platform backgrounds."

Boards need members who can:
- Challenge management on AI strategy
- Evaluate AI risk assessments
- Understand AI governance frameworks
- Ask the right questions about AI deployment

## The Indonesian SOE Governance Context

### Unique Challenges

Indonesian SOEs face governance complications that compound AI challenges:

**Multiple accountability structures:**
- Ministry of SOEs oversight
- Danantara holding company
- Sectoral regulators
- Political stakeholders
- Public accountability

**Ambiguous authority:**
- Who approves AI deployment?
- How does AI governance fit existing structures?
- What's the relationship between commercial and political accountability?

**Limited AI expertise:**
- Board members may lack AI literacy
- Governance frameworks designed for traditional operations
- Few precedents for AI governance in SOE context

### What SOE Leaders Need

Capability to:
- Design AI governance appropriate to SOE context
- Navigate multiple accountability structures
- Build AI literacy in boards and oversight bodies
- Create transparency about AI decision-making
- Maintain public trust while modernising

## Ethical Governance of AI

### Ethical Challenges

| **Challenge** | **Description** |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Bias | AI may reproduce or amplify existing biases |
| Transparency | AI reasoning may be opaque |
| Employment | AI may displace workers |
| Privacy | AI may require sensitive data |
| Autonomy | AI may reduce human agency |

### Governance Responses

| **Challenge** | **Governance Response** |
|---------------|-------------------------|
| Bias | Regular audits, diverse training data, impact assessment |
| Transparency | Explainability requirements, documentation |
| Employment | Transition planning, retraining investment |
| Privacy | Data governance, consent mechanisms |
| Autonomy | Human-in-loop requirements, override capability |

### The Leadership Requirement

Leaders must be able to:
- Recognise ethical dimensions of AI decisions
- Build governance that addresses ethical concerns
- Balance efficiency with ethical obligations
- Navigate stakeholder concerns about AI ethics
- Create culture of responsible AI use

## Regulatory Landscape

### Current State

AI regulation is evolving rapidly:
- EU AI Act establishes risk-based framework
- Various national approaches emerging
- Industry self-regulation in some domains
- Increasing attention from regulators globally

### Indonesian Context

Indonesia's regulatory environment for AI is developing:
- General data protection requirements
- Sectoral regulations may apply
- Government interest in AI development
- Uncertainty about future requirements

### What This Means for Leaders

SOE leaders must:
- Monitor regulatory developments
- Build flexible governance that can adapt
- Anticipate likely regulatory direction
- Position organisation for compliance
- Engage constructively with regulators

## Scenarios for Assessment

### Scenario 1: The AI Error

An AI system made a decision that harmed customers. Regulators are asking questions. How do you respond, and what governance changes do you make?

**Assessing:** Accountability thinking, governance design, regulatory navigation

### Scenario 2: Board AI Literacy

Your board wants to understand your AI strategy but lacks AI expertise. How do you build their capability to provide appropriate oversight?

**Assessing:** Communication, governance building, board engagement

### Scenario 3: Ethical Trade-Off

An AI system could significantly improve efficiency but would displace 1,000 workers. How do you make and govern this decision?

**Assessing:** Ethical reasoning, stakeholder consideration, governance process
