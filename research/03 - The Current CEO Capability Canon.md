# The Current CEO Capability Canon

## Models and Their Limits

The leadership assessment industry is a multi-billion dollar enterprise. Korn Ferry, DDI, Spencer Stuart, Heidrick & Struggles, Egon Zehnder, McKinsey, and dozens of other firms have developed competency frameworks, assessment methodologies, and capability models that purport to identify and develop effective leaders.

This document examines what these frameworks actually say, where they converge, and—critically—where they fall short for the task at hand: assessing whether leaders can navigate genuinely discontinuous change.

---

## The Major Frameworks

### Korn Ferry Leadership Architect™

Korn Ferry's framework comprises 38 behaviour-based competencies organised into 12 clusters across 4 factors. They describe it as enabling "employees of all levels within the organisation to make a meaningful contribution in a dynamic business environment."

**The Four Dimensions (KF4D):**

| **Dimension** | **Description** |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Competencies | Skills and behaviours required for success (decision quality, strategic mindset, global perspective, business insight) |
| Experiences | Assignments and challenges that build capability |
| Traits | Personality and predispositions |
| Drivers | Values and motivations |

**Sample Competencies (38 total):**
- Ensures accountability
- Decision quality
- Strategic mindset
- Global perspective
- Business insight
- Manages ambiguity
- Drives results

**The Approach:**
Korn Ferry benchmarks individuals against "Success Profiles"—data-driven profiles of what success looks like in specific roles, industries, or functions. They claim to analyse "factors known to be strong predictors of engagement, productivity, and leadership effectiveness."

### DDI (Development Dimensions International)

DDI maintains a library of 114 competencies across 10 job families, each with a development guide. Their philosophy: "Being a successful leader is about more than how smart you are or what skills you have. It's about what you do on the job and how you treat people."

**Key Elements:**
- **Success Profiles℠:** Complete picture of what makes someone successful—knowledge, experience, competencies, and personal attributes
- **Behavioural Interviewing:** Focus on past behaviour as predictor of future performance
- **Early Identifier:** Online assessment to spot future high-potential leaders earlier

**The Approach:**
DDI emphasises observable behaviour over self-reported capability. "Our well-defined competencies help you know the difference between leaders who talk about success and those who actually do it."

### Spencer Stuart

Spencer Stuart focuses on board and CEO-level assessment, with particular emphasis on:
- Board effectiveness assessment
- CEO succession planning
- Executive assessment for selection
- Cultural assessment

Their approach is more bespoke than framework-driven, emphasising contextual fit and stakeholder alignment over universal competency models.

### Heidrick & Struggles

Heidrick emphasises:
- Leadership assessment for critical roles
- Succession planning
- High-potential identification
- Team effectiveness

Their recent work emphasises AI leadership capabilities (see Egon Zehnder section in Document 01).

### McKinsey Leadership Practice

McKinsey's approach centres on:
- "Centred leadership" model (meaning, framing, connecting, engaging, energizing)
- Performance-based leadership assessment
- Cultural transformation capability
- Digital leadership quotient

## Where the Frameworks Converge

Despite different branding and methodologies, the major frameworks converge on several themes:

### 1. The Importance of Context

All sophisticated frameworks acknowledge that leadership effectiveness is contextual. What works in one environment may fail in another. This leads to "Success Profiles" (DDI, Korn Ferry) and bespoke assessment approaches (Spencer Stuart) rather than one-size-fits-all competency lists.

### 2. Behaviour Over Intent

The best frameworks emphasise observable behaviour rather than self-reported traits or intentions. DDI's explicit framing: distinguish "leaders who talk about success" from "those who actually do it."

### 3. Multiple Dimensions

All frameworks go beyond skills to include:
- Personality/traits
- Values/motivations
- Experiences
- Cognitive capability (often implicit)

### 4. Development Orientation

Frameworks typically include developmental guidance alongside assessment—recognising that the goal is not just selection but capability-building.

### 5. Data and Benchmarking

Modern frameworks emphasise data-driven benchmarking against relevant comparison groups, not absolute judgments.

---

## Where the Frameworks Fall Short

### 1. Backward-Looking by Design

The fundamental methodology—deriving competencies from successful current leaders—bakes in backward-looking bias. Academic research notes that this approach "can lead to highly reductionist results that lack generalisability and a future orientation."

As one critique observes: competency models often "neglect ethical, emotional, and social aspects of leadership" while focusing on what worked in the past rather than what will work in the future.

**The problem for Indonesia:** A framework derived from successful Western corporate CEOs may actively mislead when applied to SOE leaders navigating transformation in an emerging market context.

### 2. Weak Predictive Validity

The research on predictive validity is less robust than the confident marketing suggests. Meta-analytic research indicates that many leadership constructs "may lack incremental validity"—they don't predict outcomes much better than simpler measures.

A systematic review notes: "Despite various studies across different geographies, few empirical studies exist that test the validity and reliability of these measures."

**The honest assessment:** We are better at describing what we think good leadership looks like than at predicting who will actually perform well in novel situations.

### 3. Context Collapse

Frameworks claim to be contextual but often collapse context in practice. The same 38 competencies (Korn Ferry) or 114 competencies (DDI) get applied across radically different situations—a tech startup and a state-owned enterprise, a Western MNC and an Indonesian conglomerate.

**The problem:** Competency definitions assume shared understanding of what "strategic mindset" or "drives results" means. These concepts mean very different things in different cultural and institutional contexts.

### 4. The Assessment-Development Gap

Frameworks excel at assessment but struggle with development. Identifying that a leader lacks "learning agility" is easier than developing it. The research on malleability of core traits is thin.

**The honest position:** We can measure learning agility; we are less certain we can develop it in leaders who lack it.

### 5. Technology Treatment Is Superficial

Even updated frameworks treat AI and technology fluency as "a competency" rather than as a fundamental restructuring of how leaders operate. Having "digital fluency" as one of 38 competencies misses the scale of transformation required.

### 6. Transformation Capability Is Under-Specified

The frameworks mention change, agility, and adaptability—but they don't adequately specify what distinguishes leaders who successfully transform from those who don't. "Manages ambiguity" is a competency; understanding what actually predicts transformation success is a research question the frameworks haven't answered.

## The Academic Critique

Academic research on competency models surfaces several limitations:

### 1. Reductionism

Breaking leadership into discrete competencies loses the holistic, emergent nature of effective leadership. The whole is more than the sum of 38 (or 114) parts.

### 2. Lack of Future Orientation

"Competency models are controversial" precisely because they extrapolate from past success to future requirements. In stable environments, this works. In discontinuous change, it fails.

### 3. Situational Blindness

Early competency approaches were criticised for "failing to take proper account of situational factors." While modern frameworks claim to address this, the fundamental methodology—deriving competencies from successful individuals—doesn't adequately capture context dependency.

### 4. Limited Empirical Foundation

"Problems surface when competencies are considered the only factor in evaluating leaders' potential." The empirical foundation for many competency constructs is thinner than practitioners assume.

### 5. Validation Challenges

"In order for these benchmarks to have any level of validity, 50-100 people in that specific role must play the games." This creates fundamental challenges for unique or novel roles—like SOE leaders navigating Indonesia's triple transformation.

---

## What the Frameworks Get Right

Despite the limitations, the established frameworks offer valuable elements:

### 1. Structured Assessment

Any structured assessment outperforms unstructured judgment. The frameworks provide rigour, even if imperfect.

### 2. Common Language

Competency frameworks create shared vocabulary for discussing leadership requirements. This enables clearer communication about expectations and gaps.

### 3. Behavioural Specificity

The best frameworks define competencies in behavioural terms—what people actually do, not what they say or believe. This enables more reliable assessment.

### 4. Developmental Scaffolding

Linking assessment to development pathways creates actionable output, not just evaluation.

### 5. Benchmarking Infrastructure

Large firms have accumulated substantial benchmarking data, enabling comparison against relevant populations.

## Implications for Our Approach

### What to Borrow

1. **Structured methodology** — Any framework beats no framework
2. **Behavioural focus** — Assess what people do, not what they claim
3. **Multi-dimensional view** — Include traits, drivers, experiences, not just skills
4. **Development orientation** — Assessment should inform development, not just selection

### What to Reject

1. **Universal competency lists** — Context matters; Indonesia is not America
2. **Backward-looking derivation** — Future requirements may differ from past success factors
3. **Single-assessment reliance** — Triangulate across methods
4. **Technology as "a competency"** — It's a transformation, not a skill

### What to Add

1. **Transformation-specific constructs** — Learning agility, cognitive flexibility, tolerance for ambiguity
2. **AI paradigm literacy** — Understanding Anthropic vs OpenAI philosophies, agent orchestration, governance implications
3. **Hybrid workforce capability** — Leading humans and machines simultaneously
4. **Cultural context integration** — Asian leadership models, SOE-specific factors
5. **Predictive focus** — Assess trajectory, not just current position

## A Framework Comparison

| **Framework** | **Strengths** | **Limitations for Our Purpose** |
|---------------|---------------|--------------------------------|
| **Korn Ferry** | Comprehensive, data-rich, validated across populations | Western-derived, backward-looking, weak on transformation |
| **DDI** | Behavioural focus, practical development guidance | Generic competencies, limited cultural adaptation |
| **Spencer Stuart** | Bespoke, board-level expertise | Methodology opaque, limited scalability |
| **Heidrick** | Executive focus, AI awareness emerging | Recent AI work not yet validated |
| **Egon Zehnder** | Strong AI leadership framework | Still early, limited application data |
| **McKinsey** | Strategic orientation, transformation focus | Consultant-delivered, not assessment methodology |

---

## The Honest Position

The established frameworks provide useful scaffolding but are insufficient for our purpose. They were built for different contexts (Western MNCs), different challenges (incremental improvement), and different assumptions (stable environments).

Assessing whether Indonesian SOE leaders can navigate transformation to financial centre, manufacturing hub, and corporate centre simultaneously requires:
- Borrowing the rigour of established methodologies
- Rejecting their cultural assumptions and backward-looking bias
- Building transformation-specific constructs
- Integrating AI and hybrid workforce capability
- Validating in context, not just applying Western benchmarks
