# The Transformation Chasm

## Why Most Leaders Won't Cross—and What Separates Those Who Do

The statistics on transformation failure are so consistent they've become cliché: 70% fail (McKinsey), 88% fall short of original ambitions (Bain), 77% of change projects in Germany fail (Kienbaum). The exact number matters less than the pattern—the majority of transformations fail to achieve their stated goals, and most leaders cannot navigate discontinuous change.

This document examines why transformation is so difficult, what actually separates leaders who succeed from those who fail, and what this means for assessing Indonesian SOE leaders who must navigate genuinely unprecedented change.

---

## The Nature of the Chasm

### Technical vs. Adaptive Challenges

Ronald Heifetz's distinction is perhaps the most important in leadership research:

| **Technical Challenges** | **Adaptive Challenges** |
|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| Well-defined problems | Ambiguous, evolving problems |
| Solvable with existing knowledge | Require new learning and behaviour change |
| Expert intervention works | People affected must be part of solution |
| Problem and solution can be separated | People are simultaneously problem and solution |
| Relatively quick resolution | Takes far longer than expected |

**The central thesis:** The most common leadership failure consists of applying technical solutions to adaptive challenges.

**In practice:** A company rolls out SAFe (technical intervention) when the real problem is that managers hoard decisions because their identity depends on being "the one who decides" (adaptive challenge). Or: a new CRM is introduced (technical) while sales teams refuse to share customer data because it threatens their personal relationship capital (adaptive).

Most transformation efforts underestimate the adaptive component. Leaders comfortable solving technical problems apply their expertise—and fail.

### Why Transformation Is Different

Transformation differs from operational improvement in fundamental ways:

| **Operational Improvement** | **Transformation** |
|-----------------------------|-------------------|
| Make existing model work better | Change the model itself |
| Continuity with the past | Discontinuity with the past |
| Expertise is an asset | Expertise may be liability |
| Past success predicts future success | Past success may predict future failure |
| Identity-confirming | Identity-threatening |

Leaders who excelled in operational improvement may be precisely wrong for transformation. Their pattern-matching—"this is what worked before"—leads them to apply obsolete solutions to novel problems.

## Six Failure Patterns

Research and practice converge on six patterns that explain why transformations fail:

### Pattern 1: Technical Solutions to Adaptive Problems

Leaders default to what they know. Engineers solve with engineering. Finance leaders solve with restructuring. IT leaders solve with technology. But when the challenge is adaptive—requiring changes in values, beliefs, and behaviour—technical interventions produce only cosmetic change.

**The Indonesian SOE risk:** New governance structures, reorganisations, and technology platforms without addressing underlying patronage dynamics, political dependencies, and cultural expectations.

### Pattern 2: Change Fatigue as Systemic Overload

Change fatigue is not individual weakness—it's cumulative organisational condition. Employee willingness to support organisational change dropped from 74% (2016) to 43% (2022). Meanwhile, planned changes per employee rose from 2 to 10.

**The paradox:** Organisations respond to failed transformation with more transformation. Each new initiative starts not from zero but from deficit—cynicism, passive resistance, quiet disengagement.

**The Indonesian SOE risk:** Multiple overlapping reform initiatives (Danantara, sectoral reorganisation, governance reform, digital transformation) exhausting organisational capacity before any single initiative completes.

### Pattern 3: Middle Management Identity Crisis

One in three leaders experiences identity crisis during transformation—feeling burdened and uncertain about their role. Middle managers built careers on title, direct reports, and decision-making authority. Transformation threatens all three.

**The distinction:** Change is situational and external. Transition is psychological and internal. Organisations manage change but ignore transition.

**The Indonesian SOE risk:** Leaders whose authority derives from political connections and hierarchical position losing those foundations without developing new ones.

### Pattern 4: The Strategy-Execution Gap

Top-down transformation generates compliance, not commitment. The higher the hierarchical level, the more optimistic the self-assessment. Boards systematically overestimate transformation capability; project managers see reality more soberly.

**The budget tells the truth:** 80-90% of transformation budgets flow to technology. Under 2% goes to capability building—reskilling, coaching, process redesign. Yet initiatives with excellent change management are six times more likely to succeed.

**The Indonesian SOE risk:** Massive investment in infrastructure and technology without corresponding investment in leadership capability development.

### Pattern 5: Cultural Resistance as Rational Response

Resistance is multifactorial: routine-seeking, emotional reaction, cognitive rigidity, short-term focus. Framing resistance as "obstacle to overcome" is itself a failure pattern.

**The insight:** Resistance is information. It signals where the adaptive challenge lives. It reveals where identity threat exists. It shows where structural preconditions for change are absent.

**The Indonesian SOE risk:** Treating resistance as political opposition to be overcome rather than diagnostic information about what actually needs to change.

### Pattern 6: Declaring Victory Too Early

Quick wins generate momentum but also produce dopamine hit for leadership. The hard structural work feels like diminishing returns by comparison.

**The paradox:** 90% of transformation value is created by less than 5% of roles. Quick wins typically come from the other 95%—visible but not transformative.

**The Indonesian SOE risk:** Celebrating early reorganisation wins while underlying capability gaps remain unaddressed.

## What Separates Leaders Who Cross

The research on transformation capability is more modest but more rigorous than the grand competency frameworks. Several factors consistently distinguish successful transformers:

### 1. Cognitive Flexibility Under Pressure

The ability to update mental models when evidence contradicts them—and to do so under stress. This is distinct from general intelligence. It's specifically:
- Recognising when existing approaches are failing
- Generating alternative interpretations of the same evidence
- Shifting strategy without identity threat
- Maintaining effectiveness under ambiguity

**Assessment implication:** Test cognitive flexibility with scenarios that require abandoning initial hypotheses, not just solving well-defined problems.

### 2. Genuine Tolerance for Ambiguity

Many leaders claim comfort with ambiguity but demonstrate the opposite under pressure—defaulting to false certainty, premature closure, or analysis paralysis.

**Genuine tolerance shows up as:**
- Sustained decision-making in low-information environments
- Ability to act while acknowledging uncertainty
- Not requiring external validation to proceed
- Comfort with "good enough" decisions when perfect information is unavailable

**Assessment implication:** Observe behaviour under genuine uncertainty, not self-reported comfort with ambiguity.

### 3. Learning Agility That Actually Predicts

Learning agility—the ability to learn from experience and apply that learning to new situations—predicts promotability and post-promotion performance. But it's not a single trait. It includes:

| **Component** | **Description** |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Mental agility | Cognitive flexibility, comfort with complexity |
| People agility | Understanding and relating to diverse others |
| Change agility | Willingness to lead change, comfort with ambiguity |
| Results agility | Delivering under challenging, first-time conditions |
| Self-awareness | Accurate assessment of own strengths and limitations |

**Assessment implication:** Learning agility can be measured, but requires disaggregation into components for actionable insight.

### 4. Adaptive Authority

The ability to exercise different leadership styles based on context:
- Directive when clarity is needed
- Participative when commitment is needed
- Delegative when capability exists
- Switching fluidly based on situation and stakeholder

**The key distinction:** This is not "being flexible"—it's accurately reading what each situation requires and having the range to deliver it.

### 5. Identity Security

Leaders whose self-concept is threatened by change resist it, often unconsciously. Leaders with secure identities can absorb role changes without defensive reactions.

**What this looks like:**
- Willingness to acknowledge mistakes
- Ability to give credit to others
- Comfort with being wrong
- Not requiring positional authority for influence
- Learning orientation over performance orientation

**Assessment implication:** Identity security is observable in how leaders respond to challenge, feedback, and change—not in what they say about themselves.

### 6. Systemic Thinking

The ability to see how elements connect—technology, talent, strategy, culture—and understand that intervening in one area affects others.

**What this looks like:**
- Anticipating second and third-order effects
- Understanding stakeholder interdependencies
- Recognising when technical solutions create adaptive problems
- Designing interventions that address root causes, not symptoms

## The Transformation Capability Paradox

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **Leaders selected for current-state excellence are systematically unlikely to have transformation capability.**

The traits that enable success in stable environments—pattern-matching from past experience, decisive action, confidence in one's expertise—work against transformation success.

| **Current-State Excellence** | **Transformation Capability** |
|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Applies proven solutions | Invents new solutions |
| High confidence | Productive uncertainty |
| Clear mental models | Updatable mental models |
| Identity tied to role | Identity beyond role |
| Expert authority | Learning authority |

**The selection problem:** Organisations promote leaders who excel in the current state, then expect them to transform to a different state. This is asking leaders to undermine the foundation of their own success.

## Can Transformation Capability Be Developed?

The honest answer: **Partially, and not quickly.**

### What Can Be Developed

| **Capability** | **Development Path** |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Cognitive flexibility | Practice with feedback in novel situations |
| Systemic thinking | Exposure to complex systems, coaching |
| Adaptive leadership range | Deliberate practice across contexts |
| Self-awareness | 360 feedback, coaching, reflection |

### What Is Harder to Develop

| **Trait** | **Challenge** |
|-----------|---------------|
| Tolerance for ambiguity | Deep personality trait, slow to change |
| Identity security | Foundational psychological structure |
| Learning orientation | Core motivational pattern |
| General mental ability | Relatively stable in adulthood |

### The Development Honest Position

Leaders with high potential but current gaps can develop—given:
- Significant time investment (years, not months)
- Intensive support (coaching, feedback)
- Stretch assignments that build capability
- Protected space to fail and learn

Leaders with fundamental trait limitations may not develop transformation capability regardless of investment. Assessment should distinguish between "capable with development" and "unlikely to develop."

## Implications for Assessment

### What We Must Measure

1. **Cognitive flexibility** — Does the leader update beliefs when evidence contradicts them?
2. **Genuine ambiguity tolerance** — Does the leader function effectively without certainty?
3. **Learning agility components** — Mental, people, change, results, self-awareness
4. **Identity security** — Does change threaten the leader's sense of self?
5. **Systems thinking** — Does the leader see connections and anticipate effects?

### How to Measure It

| **Capability** | **Method** |
|----------------|-----------|
| Cognitive flexibility | Scenario exercises requiring belief revision |
| Ambiguity tolerance | Decision-making under genuine uncertainty |
| Learning agility | Structured behavioural interview + psychometrics |
| Identity security | 360 feedback comparing self-assessment to others' |
| Systems thinking | Complex case analysis, stakeholder mapping |

### What to Avoid

- Self-reported adaptability (everyone claims it)
- Past performance in stable conditions (poor predictor)
- Confidence and presence (often inverse predictor)
- Credential accumulation (signals compliance, not capability)

## The Chasm for Indonesian SOE Leaders

Indonesian SOE leaders face transformation challenges that compound the normal difficulty:

### Multiple Simultaneous Transformations
- Financial centre development
- Manufacturing hub development
- Corporate centre development
- Digital transformation
- Governance reform (Danantara)
- Generational workforce shift
- AI/automation integration

### Political Complexity
- Transformation timelines exceed political cycles
- Leadership tenure depends on political factors
- Accountability structures are ambiguous
- Personal relationships affect institutional decisions

### Cultural Context
- High power distance affects change dynamics
- Collective orientation shapes resistance patterns
- Relationship-based authority challenged by performance demands
- Historical patterns of patronage persist

### The Assessment Challenge

We must identify leaders who can:
- Navigate political complexity while driving transformation
- Maintain long-term focus despite short tenure expectations
- Build institutional capability while navigating relational dynamics
- Lead both technical and adaptive change simultaneously
- Develop others while developing themselves

This is an extraordinarily demanding profile. The assessment must be honest about how few leaders will genuinely possess it—and what that means for Indonesia's ambitions.
