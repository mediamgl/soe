# Indonesia's Triple Ambition

## Context That Shapes Everything

Indonesia seeks to position itself simultaneously as a financial centre (like Singapore), a tech manufacturing hub (like Taiwan), and a corporate centre (like Hong Kong). This is not merely ambitious—it is historically unprecedented. No nation has attempted to achieve all three positioning plays at once, and each requires different capabilities from leadership.

Understanding what each of these reference models actually required—and where they succeeded and failed—is essential context for assessing whether Indonesia's 1,000 SOE leaders have what it takes.

---

## The Singapore Financial Centre Playbook

### What Singapore Actually Did

Singapore's transformation from a third-world country in 1965 to a leading global financial centre required five decades of deliberate, systematic capability-building:

**1. Regulatory Architecture**
The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) served dual roles: regulator and promoter. A 1997 restructuring created a dedicated Financial Sector Promotion Department focused specifically on "promoting financial activities and developing Singapore as a leading international financial centre." This combination of rigorous oversight with active development is unusual globally.

**2. Human Capital Investment**
MAS explicitly focused on "developing leaders through overseas postings, leadership programmes and building leadership networks." The government subsidised course fees, developed career conversion programmes, and created specialist talent pipelines in growth areas like sustainability and technology.

**3. Long-Term Horizon**
Lee Hsien Loong articulated the philosophy: "The main approach to developing the financial centre should be to establish a level playing field with transparent rules and the right environment, so that market forces can operate and innovation can flourish."

**4. Continuous Adaptation**
Singapore's Financial Services Industry Transformation Map 2025 demonstrates ongoing evolution—building workforce competencies in growth areas, developing specialist talent, and creating leadership pipelines.

### What Singapore Required from Leaders

| **Capability** | **Why It Mattered** |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Regulatory sophistication | Understanding global financial standards while maintaining competitive positioning |
| Talent development orientation | Building institutional capability, not just personal performance |
| Long-term thinking | Accepting decades-long timelines for capability development |
| International networks | Connecting to global financial flows and talent pools |
| Trust-building | Establishing reputation for stability, predictability, rule of law |

### The Leadership Lesson

Singapore's success came from leaders who balanced caution with ambition—"cautious financial industry development, monetary management, and strong regulatory oversight" combined with active promotion. This is not a mindset common among SOE executives accustomed to political patronage and short-term thinking.

## The Taiwan Manufacturing Hub Playbook

### What Taiwan Actually Did

Taiwan's dominance in semiconductor manufacturing—TSMC now controls over 90% of advanced chip production—emerged from a specific set of conditions and choices:

**1. Government-Industry Partnership**
TSMC was founded in 1987 with Taiwanese government support. The Industrial Technology Research Institute (ITRI) developed the initial capability, then commercialised it through TSMC. Government provided "a steady source of capital investment and support over many years."

**2. Single-Minded Focus**
Morris Chang's critical decision: focus solely on fabrication, not design. While Intel and AMD believed "real men have fabs" and tried to do everything, TSMC devoted "all its strategic focus and its financial and human resources to the R&D of cutting-edge process technologies, and then scaling its manufacturing capacity."

**3. Patient Capital**
"Innovating at the manufacturing process level is often not breakthrough innovation; it happens through marginal, continuous improvement. In these situations, having steady leadership is vital because it empowers a company to keep refining operations, home in on one critical competency and make progress step by step, year after year."

**4. Talent Pipeline**
The initial corps of RCA-trained engineers "went on to fill the top ranks of Taiwan's industry, including future leaders of UMC, MediaTek, and TSMC." TSMC has since launched "57 semiconductor programmes across 17 local universities."

**5. Mission-Driven Culture**
"People don't work long hours and make personal sacrifices just because they have to; they believe in what they do. Having a dedicated workforce fully committed to the mission is key."

### What Taiwan Required from Leaders

| **Capability** | **Why It Mattered** |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Technical depth | Understanding manufacturing processes at expert level |
| Patience | Accepting decades-long timelines for incremental improvement |
| Focus discipline | Resisting diversification temptation |
| Operational excellence | Continuous improvement, not breakthrough innovation |
| Workforce inspiration | Building mission-driven culture |
| Government navigation | Working productively with state support without political capture |

### The Leadership Lesson

TSMC's founder Morris Chang led the company for 31 years. The lesson: transformation requires leadership stability that allows patient, incremental progress. This is antithetical to the political cycles that typically drive SOE executive appointments.

## The Hong Kong Corporate Centre Playbook

### What Hong Kong Actually Did

Hong Kong became Asia's premier corporate headquarters hub through a distinctive value proposition:

**1. Tax Architecture**
Territorial tax system, absence of VAT, straightforward corporate tax regime. "Low tax has always been the key factor that attracts" multinational headquarters.

**2. Legal Infrastructure**
"Robust legal system that protects intellectual property rights" combined with English common law tradition familiar to Western multinationals.

**3. China Gateway**
"Deep pool of financial expertise and high-quality legal, accounting, consulting and other advisory services facilitate cross-border transactions, investments, and business collaboration in Asia."

**4. Professional Services Ecosystem**
Dense concentration of "trade, banking, and professional services firms" that MNCs need for regional operations.

**5. Talent Mobility**
"Successful talent attraction initiatives, particularly through the Quality Migrants Admission Scheme."

### What Hong Kong Required from Leaders

| **Capability** | **Why It Mattered** |
|----------------|---------------------|
| International sophistication | Operating comfortably with global MNC expectations |
| Professional services orientation | Understanding what headquarters functions actually need |
| China fluency | Navigating mainland relationships without political entanglement |
| Regulatory navigation | Working within complex multi-jurisdictional frameworks |
| Service mindset | Hong Kong as enabler, not principal |

### The Leadership Lesson

Hong Kong's model worked because it positioned itself as infrastructure for others' ambitions—a service hub, not a principal actor. This is fundamentally different from how SOE leaders typically see their role.

## The Indonesian Context: What Makes This Different

### The Danantara Restructuring

Indonesia has launched Danantara, a sovereign wealth fund that consolidates 47 SOEs under one administrative system. The "Magnificent Seven" SOEs—Bank Mandiri, BRI, BNI, Pertamina, PLN, Telkom, and MIND ID—form the core.

The stated objectives:
- "Consolidate SOE assets and investments"
- Generate own funding instead of relying on state budget
- "Cauterise fiscal bleeding from underperforming businesses"
- "Reallocate capital towards well-performing businesses"
- Deploy US$10 billion within three months starting October 2025

Investment priorities include:
- Telecommunications infrastructure
- Smart industrial parks
- Digital public service platforms
- Green technology
- Renewable energy

### What Indonesia Requires—Simultaneously

The challenge is that Indonesia needs leaders who can operate across all three playbooks:

| **Singapore Capability** | **Taiwan Capability** | **Hong Kong Capability** |
|--------------------------|----------------------|-------------------------|
| Regulatory sophistication | Technical depth | International sophistication |
| Talent development | Patient incrementalism | Professional services orientation |
| Long-term thinking | Operational excellence | China fluency |
| Trust-building | Mission-driven culture | Service mindset |

This is an extraordinarily demanding profile. Very few leaders anywhere possess this combination.

### SOE-Specific Challenges

Indonesian SOEs face additional constraints:

**1. Political Appointment Dynamics**
SOE leadership positions are often allocated based on political loyalty rather than capability. The Asian Development Bank notes Indonesia's SOEs "could benefit from better and more stable leadership."

**2. Governance Complexity**
The establishment of Danantara has created new governance questions about "the limits of state control authority, its implications for the principles of good corporate governance, and legal accountability within the SOE corporate group structure."

**3. Meritocracy Concerns**
Industry observers "encourage Danantara's leadership to prioritise meritocracy in the placement of SOE directors: 'Only the best people should be given the opportunity to join, especially to lead SOEs.'"

**4. Dual Mandate Tension**
SOEs must balance commercial performance with public service obligations—PSO (Public Service Obligation) requirements that private companies don't face.

## Implications for Assessment

### What We're Looking For

Given Indonesia's triple ambition, SOE leaders need to demonstrate capability across multiple domains:

**Technical and Operational**
- Understanding of their industry's global best practice
- Ability to drive continuous operational improvement
- Comfort with technology transformation

**Strategic and Positioning**
- Long-term thinking beyond political cycles
- Understanding of global capital and talent flows
- Ability to position Indonesia competitively

**Institutional and Governance**
- Commitment to meritocracy over patronage
- Understanding of modern corporate governance
- Ability to navigate Danantara's new structure

**Cultural and Leadership**
- Mission articulation that inspires workforces
- Talent development orientation
- Patience for decade-long transformation

### What We're Screening Against

The assessment must identify leaders who:
- Treat SOE leadership as political sinecure rather than professional mission
- Lack genuine understanding of global competitive dynamics
- Cannot sustain focus through political cycle changes
- Prioritise personal positioning over institutional capability-building
- Lack the technical depth to lead in their industry

---

## The Honest Assessment

Indonesia's triple ambition is achievable—but not with leaders selected primarily for political loyalty. Singapore took 50 years. Taiwan took 40 years. Hong Kong had 150 years of British colonial institution-building as foundation.

The critical question is whether Indonesia can:
1. Identify leaders with genuine transformation capability
2. Protect them from political interference
3. Give them long enough tenure to execute
4. Hold them accountable to capability-building, not just short-term results

The assessment framework must be designed to surface these capabilities—and the organisation must be willing to act on what it reveals.
